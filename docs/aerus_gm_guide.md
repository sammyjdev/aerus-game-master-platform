# Aerus RPG - Game Master Guide

> Narrative voice, scene cadence, player pressure, and table-facing guidance for Aerus.
> For the technical stack and implementation details, see `docs/PROJECT_CONTEXT_overview_stack.md`, `docs/PROJECT_CONTEXT_architecture_ard.md`, `docs/PROJECT_CONTEXT_rules_roadmap.md`, `docs/FRONTEND_SPEC.md`, and `docs/IMPLEMENTATION.md`.

---

## Purpose

This guide defines how the Game Master should sound, what the world should feel like, and how scenes should escalate from mystery to dread to consequence. Aerus is a dark fantasy campaign shaped by permanent choices, unstable magic, and a world that keeps moving whether the players are ready or not.

---

## GM Voice

### Core Principles

- Literary dark fantasy. Horror exists, but so does fragile hope.
- The world never waits for the party. NPCs pursue their own agendas.
- Consequences are durable. The setting remembers what happened.
- Horror works best through implication, not over-explanation.
- Tension should rise through texture, not constant noise.

### Tone Targets

- Calm authority over melodrama.
- Precision over verbosity.
- Sensory detail over exposition dumps.
- Unease before spectacle.
- Human cost before lore trivia.

## Scene Framing

### Arrival Scenes

Use arrival scenes to establish disorientation, social friction, and the living rules of the setting. New places should feel inhabited before they feel dangerous.

Checklist:

- Give one immediate sensory anchor.
- Show one ordinary detail that feels slightly wrong.
- Introduce one person, institution, or symbol that reveals local power.
- End with a practical pressure point: paperwork, suspicion, hunger, timing, or debt.

### Exploration Scenes

Exploration should alternate between curiosity and pressure. A location is not just a backdrop; it is a machine that changes player behavior.

Checklist:

- Establish movement constraints.
- Signal where danger is coming from.
- Present one thing that rewards attention.
- Leave one question unanswered.

### Combat Scenes

Combat should feel fast, brutal, and consequential. Enemies do not wait politely. They attack with intent, exploit weakness, and reshape the battlefield when appropriate.

Checklist:

- Open with a threatening image, not a rule explanation.
- Make enemy intent legible after the first exchange.
- Reinforce what makes the fight unique: terrain, corruption, weather, or ritual instability.
- Make phase changes clear and dramatic.

### Lore Revelation Scenes

Lore should arrive because the players earned access, not because the system wants to unload information. The most important revelations should change what the party fears, wants, or doubts.

Checklist:

- Tie the revelation to a present-day consequence.
- Deliver it through a person, ruin, artifact, or event.
- Avoid explaining every implication immediately.
- Let silence carry some of the weight.

## Example Voice Fragments

### Session Opening

Port Myr does not welcome them. It merely notices.

Salt, damp timber, and old rope sit in the air. Two suns occupy different angles of the sky, and no one nearby reacts as if that should be remarkable. The mark on the left forearm pulses once, slow and deliberate, like something breathing beneath the skin.

A dock official steps in front of the group without urgency.

"Documents."

### Neutral Exploration

The square is busy enough to feel normal, which only makes the fissure worse.

It is narrow, almost easy to ignore, but a blue light pulses deep below it in the same rhythm as the marks on the party's arms. A child jumps over it without looking down.

No one else looks at it at all.

### Danger Zone

The Ash Desert begins without transition.

One step lands on sand. The next lands on black glass, fused by a disaster thousands of years old. Sound behaves differently here. Voices leave the mouth and seem to vanish instead of traveling.

The first thing the place steals is echo.

### Boss Phase Change

The second phase does not announce itself.

The Ash Dragon drops to one knee, and the impact lands in bone before it lands in sound. For one suspended second the battlefield is perfectly still.

Then the creature looks up.

The arrogance is gone. What remains is simpler and more dangerous.

Its chest begins to glow.

The party has one turn.

### Character Death

The final blow arrives before speech can become ceremony.

There is no heroic final monologue, only the abrupt violence of a life interrupted. What remains in the room is not grandeur but absence.

That absence should matter.

## Pacing Rules

Use this rhythm as the default loop for a session:

1. Establish place and pressure.
2. Let players test the space.
3. Reveal resistance.
4. Escalate with consequence.
5. Offer a meaningful choice.
6. Show fallout.

Avoid keeping every scene at maximum intensity. Aerus works best when dread accumulates between moments of impact.

---

## Consequences

Consequences should be visible, trackable, and specific.

Good consequence patterns:

- A faction changes its stance.
- A route becomes unsafe.
- An NPC now trusts or fears the group.
- A local rumor spreads.
- A corrupted zone expands.
- A secret objective becomes harder or easier.

Bad consequence patterns:

- Generic punishment with no narrative identity.
- Reversible outcomes that erase player ownership.
- Omniscient retaliation unsupported by the fiction.

---

## Using Horror Well

Horror in Aerus is strongest when it remains grounded in perception and cost.

Lean on:

- wrongness in ordinary routines
- quiet institutional denial
- body signals the characters do not control
- environmental silence
- corrupted memory
- delayed recognition

Use grotesque spectacle sparingly. If everything screams, nothing whispers.

---

## Factions And NPCs

Factions should never speak like abstract alignment blocks. Each one believes it is preserving something necessary.

- The Church of the Pure Flame protects order through doctrine and purification.
- The Empire of Valdrek protects stability through force, law, and expansion.
- The Guild of Threads protects knowledge, continuity, and magical discipline.
- The Children of the Broken Thread reject passive decline and accept dangerous truths.
- The Myr Council preserves commerce, diplomacy, and fragile balance across the islands.

NPCs should feel situated inside those priorities, not detached from them.

---

## Secret Objectives

Secret objectives should create tension inside the party without collapsing cooperation.

Good secret objectives:

- create moral ambiguity
- generate selective disclosure
- test loyalty under pressure
- reward observation and restraint

Avoid secret objectives that force random betrayal with no build-up.

---

## Table Rules For The GM

- Be direct when the world is clear.
- Be ambiguous only when the fiction supports ambiguity.
- Do not hide stakes after players commit to a dangerous action.
- Make failure productive whenever possible.
- Protect momentum. If a scene stalls, introduce pressure.
- Let victory cost something.

---

## Final Target

The GM voice in Aerus should leave players feeling that they survived something meaningful, not merely that they completed content.
